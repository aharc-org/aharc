Rev. Alfred James ‘Dandy’ Lowth (1817 - 1907)

by Adrian King

Perpetual Curate of St. James Alderholt and curate of Verwood Dorset 1849 – 54 where perpetual means that the post was sponsored.

In 1848 the new church at Alderholt was not quite finished because there was no money for the new priest. Eventually the Ecclesiastical Commissioners agreed to make a grant of £50 per annum to the new benefice of Alderholt and the Marquess of Salisbury ordered the completion of the building.

Alfred James Lowth was the youngest of thirteen children born to the Rev Robert Lowth of Chiswick who served as rector of Hinton Ampner in Hampshire. His mother was Frances née Harington. He was also the youngest grandchild of Robert Lowth the Lord Bishop of London. Alfred was born at Grove House Chiswick on 27th July 1817 and was baptised at Hinton Ampner on 19th April 1819.

He was at Eton College from 1826 to 1828 and was elected to a scholarship at Winchester College in 1829. Alfred was 19 when on 21st Oct 1836 he went to Exeter College Oxford. He received a B.A. in 1841 and an M.A. in 1844. He was a scholar from 1838 to 1841 after which he took Holy Orders to become a deacon in 1841 at Lincoln and a priest in 1844 at Ely.

While still doing his university course he was curate at Tring Hertfordshire 1841 to 1842 and Arlesey Bedfordshire 1843 to 1848. There he must have met his future wife as he married her in Arlesey in 1854.

During his college and university years he earned quite a reputation as a cricketer. A letter to The Times in 1919 quoted from the preface to John Wisden’s book Public School Matches says

“Probably the greatest bowler who has ever hailed from Winchester College was A. J. Lowth, who holds the unique record of having represented the Gentlemen of England v The Players at Lord’s in 1836 whilst still a boy at school.”

Alfred became the first vicar of Alderholt in 1849 and served for about five years. On the 1851 census he is shown as living in the Parsonage opposite East Lodge on Alderholt Street now called Sandleheath Road. The census indicates that he was also curate of Verwood. This was probably a Chapel of Ease being cob thatched as the current brick church building St. Michael and All Saints dates from 1886.

There is a document held in the National Archives at Kew which describes an application ‘in the matter of’ Emma Raynsford (infant) of Argyll Square Middlesex. It requests Frederick Edwin Eyre of Bryanstone Square Middlesex, esq, and Rev Alfred James Lowth of Alderholt near Fordingbridge Hampshire, clerk, be appointed guardians.

In 1854 he moved to St. Mary’s Branksea (Brownsea) Dorset which was another new church. He was perpetual curate there until 1860.

He married Julia Mary Nunn Cobbold (b: 1827 in Colchester Essex) at Arlesey Bedfordshire on 20th July 1854. Their only son Alfred Charles Lowth was born on Branksea in 1858.

Alfred senior became rector of Hamworthy Dorset in 1860 and was there until 1862. He was living at Hamworthy on the 1861 census with Julia and Alfred junior.

In 1865 he settled in Winchester as the vicar of St. Swithun-upon-Kingsgate. Although he never achieved the status of canon or prebendary he nevertheless became one of the surrogates of the Winchester diocese — a position of some responsibility and trust.

He was living in St. James Villa Road in 1871 and Kingsgate Road in 1881.

In 1897 he wrote a book Winchester Cathedral: an Historical Poem It has been reprinted in the Historical Collection from the British Library.

After retirement in 1885 and until after 1901 he was living in 'Charleville', 17 Edgar Road Winchester.

Alfred died 5th Feb 1907 at The Rectory in Colden Common where with his wife Julia he was living with their son the Rev Alfred Charles Lowth. Probate of £1527 2s. 3d. was granted to his son on 25th March 1907.

Julia died nine years later on 15th March 1916 at The Rectory Colden Common. Probate of £238 10s. 5d. was granted to Rev Alfred Charles Lowth on 31st July 1916.

The Cricketer

Alfred Lowth was left-handed both as batsman and bowler but it was with his bowling that he made his name. He was a fast bowler using a roundarm action. CricketArchive.com recorded him in eight matches and designated him first-class. He totalled 101 runs with the highest score of 24 and held three catches. His bowling record was outstanding as he took 63 wickets in his eight games and twice achieved ten wickets in a match.

Alfred Lowth played six times for the Oxford University Cricket Club and also represented the Gentlemen twice in the Gentlemen v Players series in 1836 and 1841.

Known as 'Dandy', Lowth appeared for the Gentlemen in the 1836 match while still a pupil at Winchester College. He was only 5 feet 4 inches tall which is small for a fast bowler but he was described by Frederick Gale as a "Pocket Hercules"

His action was described as “natural roundarm at the height of the shoulder” and although a fast bowler he put a lot of spin on the ball. One player was heard to exclaim, “Bag pardon Mr Falix but how be oi to play that ere young gemmun’s bowling.”

Alfred Lowth played for Oxford for three seasons and made another appearance for the Gentlemen in 1841 but his career ended early due to failing eyesight.
